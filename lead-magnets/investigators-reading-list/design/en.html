Not believer. Not skeptic. Investigator.

Every study in this list circles back to the same question: how far does the mind reach? Princeton tested it for 28 years. The CIA for 23. A laboratory at Mexico's UNAM in 1994. A Dutch hospital in 2014 and again in 2022. The answers don't fit a 30-second clip — they fit a methodology, a sample size, and a published paper. Which is what this document gives you.

The point isn't to convince you of anything. It's to put the actual experiments in your hands — peer-reviewed papers, declassified programs, university archives — so you can decide what the data shows. Without intermediaries. Without overclaiming. Most consciousness research on the internet is sold louder than it was tested. This list does the opposite: under-promised, over-cited, source-linked.

Each entry has the same shape. One plain-language paragraph of what was done. One full source citation with author, year, and journal. One italic line marked Why this matters →, which states what an honest investigator should take from it. Nothing more. Nothing less.

01
1979–2007 · Princeton · United States

Princeton ran 1.7 million attention experiments for 28 years.

A Princeton engineering lab quietly tested whether human attention could nudge random number generators. They ran the experiment for nearly three decades. The accumulated effect was tiny per trial but stayed statistically positive across 1.7 million trials. The lab closed in 2007 without resolving the underlying mechanism.

Source · Jahn & Dunne · Foundations of Physics · 1986 / Final report 2007

Three decades of clean experimental design is not noise. The mechanism is open. Curiosity is the rational position.

02
2014 · The Netherlands · 24 subjects, double-blind

Dutch researchers injected 24 healthy people with a bacterial toxin.

Half received ten days of breath-training under the Wim Hof method. The trained group's bodies dampened the inflammatory response after exposure. The trial was double-blind and published in a top-tier American academy journal.

Source · Kox et al. · Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA · 111(20):7379-7384 · 2014

The body's inflammatory response was previously considered autonomic. This study showed it can be modulated by trained breathing.

03
1972–1995 · CIA · United States

The CIA ran a 23-year remote-viewing program. They closed it three months before publishing the audit.

$20 million. More than 450 operational missions. The statistician hired to review the data — later president of the American Statistical Association — concluded the effect was statistically real. The program was shut down in 1995.

Source · Utts, J. · An Assessment of the Evidence for Psychic Functioning · AIR · 1995

The closure was political, not statistical. The published data still exists.

04
1961–2007 · University of Virginia · United States

A University of Virginia psychiatrist documented 2,500 children who reported memories of previous lives.

Forty-year research program. Cases were investigated for falsifiable details — names, places, scars. Some matched previously unknown facts. The investigator was rigorous about ruling out fraud or coincidence; he did not claim proof of reincarnation, only documentation.

Source · Stevenson, I. · Children Who Remember Previous Lives · UVA Press · 1987

The investigator's job is to look at what was actually documented, not what the headline implies.

05
1994 · UNAM, Mexico · Shielded chambers

A Mexican neuropsychologist documented the same brain pattern in two separated people, after only one was stimulated.

Pairs of meditators sat in electromagnetically shielded rooms. A flash of light was delivered to one subject. The other subject's brain activity showed a correlated pattern with no physical signal between them. Published in a peer-reviewed physics journal.

Source · Grinberg-Zylberbaum et al. · Physics Essays · 7(4) 422-428 · 1994

Partially replicated since. The result is small but the methodology is auditable.

06
1991–2016 · HeartMath Institute · California

25 years measuring whether your heart and brain sync up under coherence.

Heart rate variability — the tiny beat-to-beat changes that reflect nervous-system balance — shifts measurably under specific emotional and breathing states. The institute's work is independent of academic medicine but the methodology is published.

Source · Shaffer & Ginsberg · Frontiers in Public Health · 5:258 · 2017

Heart rate variability is a documented biomarker of nervous-system state, not folklore.

07
1956–2008 · Clinical · United States & Czech Republic

Stanislav Grof logged 50 years of clinical observation of breath-induced altered states.

The holotropic-breathwork protocol produces consistent, observable shifts in conscious experience without any pharmaceutical input. Grof's clinical record is the longest single-author dataset on the topic, spanning psychiatric patients and healthy subjects across two countries.

Source · Grof, S. · Realms of the Human Unconscious · Viking Press · 1976

A 50-year first-person research record is data, even when the mechanism is not yet measured.

08
2022 · Hospital pre-surgical · The Netherlands

A 2022 hospital study reproduced the Dutch breathing experiment in pre-surgical patients.

Different sample. Different setting. Same direction of effect on inflammation markers. The 2014 result was not a fluke. The study extended the protocol from healthy volunteers to patients about to undergo surgery — a population with a real clinical stake in the outcome.

Source · Zwaag et al. · Critical Care · 26:120 · 2022

Replication is the gold standard. This one passed it.

09
1980–2003 · Cambridge / public experiments · UK

Rupert Sheldrake's crossword and "sense of being stared at" experiments.

Two decades of public, large-sample experiments testing whether information might transfer outside known channels. Effects are small but consistent across studies. Sheldrake proposed a framework he calls morphic resonance — the hypothesis that patterns of behavior can propagate non-locally — to explain the regularity.

Source · Sheldrake, R. · The Sense of Being Stared At · Crown · 2003

Small effect is not the same as no effect. Replication trumps elegance.

10
2008–present · University of California Irvine

Donald Hoffman's math says perception is fitness-tuned, not truth-tuned.

Evolutionary game theory shows organisms whose perceptions track utility outcompete organisms whose perceptions track reality. The implication: what you see is an interface, not the world. Hoffman calls it Interface Theory of Perception — a model that turns the question of "anomalous data" on its head.

Source · Hoffman, D. · The Case Against Reality · W.W. Norton · 2019

If perception is interface, then "anomalous" data may be revealing the boundary, not violating reality.

11
2004–present · University of Wisconsin-Madison

Giulio Tononi proposed a mathematical theory of when something is conscious.

Integrated Information Theory quantifies how much causally integrated information a system contains. Above a threshold, the math predicts consciousness. The theory makes testable predictions about anesthesia, brain damage, and split-brain patients — and several of those predictions have already been tested.

Source · Tononi, G. · Phi: A Voyage from the Brain to the Soul · Pantheon · 2012

A theory of consciousness with falsifiable predictions is a theory worth tracking.

12
2010–present · Independent · The Netherlands

A Dutch philosopher argues consciousness is fundamental — not produced by the brain.

Kastrup's analytic idealism inverts the standard model: matter is what consciousness looks like from outside. The position is defended philosophically, with logical arguments tested against quantum measurement, dissociative identity disorder, and near-death experience literature — not mystically.

Source · Kastrup, B. · The Idea of the World · Iff Books · 2019

Even if you reject the conclusion, the argument forces you to defend physicalism on its actual merits.

13
2021 · Brown University Medical · United States

A Harvard-trained psychologist published the first systematic study of when meditation goes wrong.

Surveyed long-term meditators and clinical practitioners. Adverse effects — insomnia, dissociation, anxiety, depersonalization — are real, under-discussed, and predictable. The investigator's stance is the honest one: the practice is powerful in both directions, and the upside literature has out-paced the downside literature by decades.

Source · Britton et al. · Clinical Psychological Science · 9(6) · 2021

Strong tools have side effects. Pretending otherwise is malpractice.

14
1978–1996 · Operational · United States

Joe McMoneagle's hit/miss record was logged across CIA missions for 18 years.

Public, declassified records show his operational accuracy in remote-viewing tasks across nearly two decades of intelligence work. Not perfect, not random — somewhere in between, consistently. The case files were eventually released and remain available in the National Archives.

Source · McMoneagle, J. · The Stargate Chronicles · Hampton Roads · 2002

The published record exists. Read it before you have an opinion.
