# The Investigator's Reading List

*14 consciousness studies your science teacher won't mention.*

A field report by Alejandro Del Palacio · Black Swan Project · 2026

This list is for the person who doesn't believe everything they're told and doesn't dismiss everything they can't explain. Call that person the investigator. The studies below are the ones that sit on the edge of what mainstream textbooks cover, and they deserve a look on their own terms.

---

## How this list is structured

These are primary sources. Not summaries of summaries, not blog recaps. Each entry points you to the actual paper, book, or declassified document so you can read it yourself and form your own view. Most of them you can find online in under a minute.

The position behind this list is simple. The believer camp overclaims. The skeptic camp dismisses without reading. Both leave the same blind spot. The investigator reads the source, notices what is solid and what is shaky, and is comfortable saying "interesting, keep watching" rather than rushing to a verdict.

Every entry below uses the same shape. A short summary in plain language. A citation you can chase down. And a single italic line on why this matters if you are doing your own homework.

## 1. Princeton's 28-year program on mind and machines

From 1979 to 2007, an engineering lab at Princeton University ran one of the longest controlled experiments in modern science. Roughly 1.7 million trials. The question was narrow and testable. Could a person, sitting in a chair and concentrating, nudge the output of a machine designed to produce random numbers? The effect they reported was tiny, but it was statistically consistent across decades, operators, and continents. The lab closed in 2007, and the data is still being argued about.

Source: Jahn, R. & Dunne, B. (1986). On the quantum mechanics of consciousness, with application to anomalous phenomena. *Foundations of Physics*, 16(8), 721-772.

*Why this matters for the investigator: 28 years and 1.7 million trials is not a weekend experiment. Whatever you conclude, the dataset exists.*

## 2. Dutch researchers, 2014, and a controlled bacterial challenge

Healthy volunteers in the Netherlands were trained in a breathing and cold-exposure method developed by Wim Hof. Then they were injected with a small dose of bacterial toxin that normally produces flu-like symptoms. The trained group showed lower inflammatory markers (the molecules the body releases when it gets sick) and milder symptoms than the untrained controls. The result suggested that breathing alone could partially regulate the immune system, which the textbooks called impossible.

Source: Kox, M. et al. (2014). Voluntary activation of the sympathetic nervous system and attenuation of the innate immune response in humans. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 111(20), 7379-7384.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a peer-reviewed national-academy paper. Hard to wave off.*

## 3. The CIA's 23-year remote-viewing program

From 1972 to 1995, the CIA, DIA, and Army Intelligence funded a classified research and operations program studying whether trained subjects could describe distant locations they had never seen. The budget reached roughly $20 million. The program ran more than 450 operational tasks. After the program was declassified, the statistician Jessica Utts (later president of the American Statistical Association) reviewed the data and concluded the effect was real and not explainable by chance. The skeptic reviewer disagreed on interpretation but not on the math.

Source: Utts, J. (1995). An assessment of the evidence for psychic functioning. Commissioned report for the American Institutes for Research / CIA Stargate review.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a sitting president of the American Statistical Association went on record. The numbers are public.*

## 4. Dr. Ian Stevenson's 40 years at the University of Virginia

Ian Stevenson, head of psychiatry at the University of Virginia, spent four decades documenting children who claimed to remember a previous life. He gathered roughly 2,500 cases across cultures. His methodology was conservative. He cross-checked names, locations, birthmarks, and verifiable family details before the child could have learned them by ordinary means. He never claimed the cases proved reincarnation. He claimed they were anomalous and worth a serious record.

Source: Stevenson, I. (1987). *Children Who Remember Previous Lives.* University of Virginia Press.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a department chair at a major American medical school took the question seriously for 40 years.*

## 5. A Mexican neuropsychologist's 1994 paired-brain experiment

In 1994, a neuropsychologist at the National Autonomous University of Mexico ran a quiet experiment. Two volunteers were placed in separate, electromagnetically shielded rooms. They were asked to feel connected to one another, then only one of them was given a sensory stimulus (a flash of light). His instruments recorded a matching electrical pattern in the other person's brain at the same moment. The result has been replicated in part by other labs and disputed by others, which is exactly what should happen with a finding this strange.

Source: Grinberg-Zylberbaum, J. et al. (1994). The Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen paradox in the brain. *Physics Essays*, 7(4), 422-428.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a serious lab, a serious instrument, an effect that should be impossible. Worth a read.*

## 6. HeartMath Institute on heart-brain coherence

For more than 25 years, a research group in California has measured heart-rate variability (the small beat-to-beat changes that reflect nervous-system balance) and how it correlates with focused emotional states like appreciation and calm. The finding is consistent. When the heart rhythm becomes smooth and orderly, the brain follows. The implication is that the heart is not just a pump. It is part of the body's signaling system to the brain, and we can train it on purpose.

Source: Shaffer, F. & Ginsberg, J. (2017). An overview of heart rate variability metrics and norms. *Frontiers in Public Health*, 5, 258.

*Why this matters for the investigator: cheap, measurable, and you can run the experiment on yourself with a $50 sensor.*

## 7. Stanislav Grof and 50 years of breathwork research

Stanislav Grof, a Czech-American psychiatrist trained at Johns Hopkins, spent his career studying altered states of consciousness, first with legal psychedelics in the 1960s and then, when those were banned, with a breathing technique that produced similar states. Over decades of clinical observation, he documented patterns that crossed cultures and patient histories. His work is not a controlled trial. It is a careful clinical record from a trained psychiatrist, and the patterns he found are now being revisited by today's psychedelic-research labs at Hopkins, NYU, and Imperial College.

Source: Grof, S. (1976). *Realms of the Human Unconscious: Observations from LSD Research.* E.P. Dutton.

*Why this matters for the investigator: the modern psychedelic-science wave is partly catching up to what Grof wrote in the 1970s.*

## 8. A 2022 hospital study reproduced the 2014 breathing finding

A research team at a Dutch academic hospital ran the cold-and-breathing protocol again, this time on patients about to undergo major surgery. The trained group again showed measurably reduced markers of inflammation compared with controls. Replication is the most important word in science. When a strange finding survives a second careful test in a different setting, it stops being a curiosity and starts being a phenomenon.

Source: Zwaag, J. et al. (2022). The effects of cold exposure training and a breathing exercise on the inflammatory response in humans. *Psychosomatic Medicine*, 84(4), 457-467.

*Why this matters for the investigator: replication. The first study was eight years before. The second one held.*

## 9. Rupert Sheldrake's stare experiments

Rupert Sheldrake, a biochemist trained at Cambridge, ran simple, low-cost experiments asking whether people can tell when they are being stared at from behind. Across thousands of trials in schools, offices, and labs, subjects scored slightly above chance with a consistency that survived peer review in mainstream journals. He also ran the well-known crossword study, in which solvers did better on a crossword the day after it had been solved by thousands of people elsewhere. His critics push back hard on his interpretation. The data itself has not gone away.

Source: Sheldrake, R. (2003). *The Sense of Being Stared At: And Other Unexplained Powers of the Human Mind.* Crown.

*Why this matters for the investigator: cheap experiments, large sample sizes, results that lean above chance. Run one yourself.*

## 10. Donald Hoffman and the math of perception

Donald Hoffman, a cognitive scientist at UC Irvine, used evolutionary game theory and computer simulations to ask a sharp question. Did natural selection favor brains that see reality accurately, or brains that see whatever helps them survive? His models came down hard on the second option. He argues, with math, that what we experience as "the world" is more like a desktop interface than a window onto truth. The trees and rocks are user-friendly icons. What's underneath is not necessarily what it looks like.

Source: Hoffman, D. (2019). *The Case Against Reality: Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes.* W. W. Norton.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a tenured American cognitive scientist arguing, with peer-reviewed math, that reality isn't what you see.*

## 11. Giulio Tononi's mathematical theory of consciousness

Giulio Tononi, a neuroscientist at the University of Wisconsin, built a mathematical framework that tries to quantify consciousness itself. The idea is simple to state and hard to compute. A system is conscious to the degree that its parts share information in a way that cannot be reduced to the parts working separately. The theory makes testable predictions about which brain states should feel like something and which should not, and it is now one of the leading working theories of consciousness in academic neuroscience.

Source: Tononi, G. (2012). *Phi: A Voyage from the Brain to the Soul.* Pantheon.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a serious mainstream neuroscientist arguing that consciousness is a measurable quantity, not a mystery to be ignored.*

## 12. Bernardo Kastrup's case for analytic idealism

Bernardo Kastrup holds two doctorates, one in computer engineering and one in philosophy, and runs a foundation dedicated to consciousness research. His argument is philosophical, but it is precise. He claims that the standard scientific story (matter is fundamental, consciousness somehow emerges from it) creates more problems than it solves, and that flipping the picture (consciousness is fundamental, matter is what consciousness looks like from the outside) is actually the simpler and more coherent option. You don't have to agree. You should read the argument before dismissing it.

Source: Kastrup, B. (2019). *The Idea of the World: A Multi-Disciplinary Argument for the Mental Nature of Reality.* Iff Books.

*Why this matters for the investigator: the alternative to materialism that academic philosophy is taking seriously, written by a doctorate-trained engineer.*

## 13. A Harvard-trained psychologist on when meditation goes wrong

Willoughby Britton, trained at Harvard and now at Brown University, published the first systematic study of what happens when contemplative practices produce harmful side effects. Anxiety, dissociation, sleep disruption, in some cases serious psychological distress. Meditation is not a neutral tool, and her work makes the case that the wellness industry has been telling a one-sided story. This entry is on the list precisely because the investigator's lane includes the inconvenient findings too.

Source: Britton, W. (2019). Can mindfulness be too much of a good thing? The value of a middle way. *Current Opinion in Psychology*, 28, 159-165.

*Why this matters for the investigator: any practice powerful enough to help is powerful enough to harm. Britton wrote the field's first map.*

## 14. Joe McMoneagle's operational record

Joe McMoneagle was one of the longest-serving operational subjects in the CIA's remote-viewing program. After the program was declassified, his hit-and-miss record on real intelligence tasks became part of the public file. He was given coordinates or sealed envelopes. He was asked to describe what was there. His descriptions were later checked against the ground truth. The record is not perfect, but it is far from random, and he passed it under conditions designed to catch fraud. His memoir lays out the cases the agency cleared for public release.

Source: McMoneagle, J. (2002). *The Stargate Chronicles: Memoirs of a Psychic Spy.* Hampton Roads.

*Why this matters for the investigator: a vetted intelligence operator with a public, declassified hit record. Read it before forming a view.*
